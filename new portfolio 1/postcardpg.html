My project will highlight the usage of city bikes worldwide and see if there's a connection between infrastructure and the use of bicycles within a city. I want to display if pedestrianisation equals more bike usage within the city. Pedestrianisation is creating an area where it is converted to areas for walking use. For the outcome, I will create hand-drawn postcards showing visual data from each city with the most bike usage to display my idea.

Final Product

The idea of using postcards stuck with me because people use postcards to share experiences and information by posting them. And allows the reader to stop and experience. In contrast, biking allows you to experience the world around you physically. I decided to base my idea on city bikes and postcards because they both use movement to connect with their surroundings and build an appreciation of the world around them. Postcards enable the transmission of personal information without relying on digital means, which aligns with my view about being present.

Research

Pedestrianisation is vital in creating an urban environment that contributes to sustainability. Creating livable cities is essential and impacts sustainability by reducing carbon emissions and allowing citizens to be healthy by walking everywhere. In the long term, it creates a better quality of life for locals and cities worldwide. They have implemented pedestrianisation in Copenhagen by making no-car zones and investments in cycling and public transport infrastructure (Yassin,2019). By creating these enforcements, Copenhagen has lowered emissions and made areas more pedestrianisation friendly. This was a driving factor for my work because it started spaces for citizens to be more environmentally sustainable and efficient mobility.

More pedestrianised areas also mean increased use of bikes, which will raise bike-sharing usage. Bike sharing can also help locals get to other closer transportation links, e.g., buses, without using cars. Biking also has health benefits since it is a physical activity. Investments in bike sharing not only helps users around the local area but keep areas empty for pedestrians (DeMario,2009). Using bicycles and pedestrians on the road can signify how well a city's infrastructure is set up. By looking at city bike data, you can see how well a city is built for mobilisation.

Process

In the project's early stages, I used Python libraries like requests, JSON, Numpy, Counter, and Excel for data scraping. I gathered data from the CityBikes API, available at http://api.citybik.es/v2/, with a focus on the 'networks' section, which provided vital information like id, country, company, and name. This step set the foundation for my analysis of bike-sharing data in different cities.

Next, I delved into a thorough examination of the countries and companies involved in the bike-sharing network. Using the Counter library, I carefully studied the countries with active bike stations. The results showed that Italy, Germany, Poland, and the United States had the most bike stations. An interesting finding was the United States' high ranking, prompting me to explore its connection to pedestrianisation efforts.

Image of Counter Data

Continuing my analysis, I observed that network names matched the operating companies. This insight led me to investigate these companies, aided by the Counter module. Notably, companies like Nextbike, Bicincitt√†, EasyBike, and VRN emerged as key players. Importantly, these companies were mostly headquartered in the countries where they operated their bike-sharing networks.

Image of Station Specific Data

Moving on to station-specific data, I collected and analysed information such as the number of stations, mean bike availability, and mean empty slots. This data provided insights into the distribution and utilisation of bike stations. In addition, I scrutinised individual stations, examining their unique attributes such as station ID, free bikes, location, and timestamps. This deeper exploration helped me understand station dynamics and usage patterns.

Google Maps Visualisation

To gain insights into pedestrianisation and its relationship with bike usage, I turned to Google Maps. This allowed me to assess the infrastructure around specific bike stations visually. Germany stood out with its robust pedestrianisation infrastructure, characterised by extensive bike lanes and proximity to amenities near stations. Italy had numerous street lanes but limited bike lanes, potentially reflecting park-centric usage. Greece boasted ample street lanes but lacked dedicated bike lanes, likely due to coastal riding preferences. Poland's infrastructure featured street and bike lanes, primarily concentrated around main roads and schools. Notably, bike station placement appeared strategically, with stations in pedestrianised areas or tourist hubs.
